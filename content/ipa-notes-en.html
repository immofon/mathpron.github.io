This article explains the narrow IPA transcriptions used in this project.

Use of IPA

IPA is a widely used alphabet for phonemic transcription, but it is inadequate when scientific precision is required. The definitions of its symbols are ambiguous, and sometimes, a symbol such as {{ipc|ə}} or {{ipc|ɹ}} can represent dozens of different sounds.

This is not a big problem, as IPA is not aimed at precise phonetic transcription. The problem is that it has to be used for such purposes, as it is the only official system. To make the situation worse, different phoneticians have very different judgements of sounds, even if they are listening to the same sound clips.{{ref|2}} This leads to a strange phenomenon: for every language in the world, phoneticians never agree on what that language’s sounds are.

This has to change, and the solution is simple. First, one needs to specify the meanings of the symbols in a precise manner, and then, one needs to train his ears to distinguish different sounds represented by different symbols. A great attempt to change the situation is made by L. Canepari, whose work{{ref|1}} is used as a guide for the phonetics part of this project. He defines each sound by the exact shape of the vocal tract, and devises a new notation system. We follow his theory, but we retain the standard IPA symbols, with a few necessary adjustments.

Notes

  • The space of all possible vowels is, mathematically speaking, infinite-dimensional. But the most important parameters are openness, backness, and rounding, as shown in the table. Other parameters can be specified using diacritics that are listed below.
  • To be precise, the rectangular shape of the table parametrizes the position of a fixed point on the tongue, located at the centre of the dorsum of the tongue. The shape of the space of vowels is nearly a parallelogram, which we turn into a rectangle linearly, and divide evenly into 7 × 5 rectangular cells. Of course, sticking out the tongue will break this parallelogram, but that is (normally) not considered as a vowel.
  • The official IPA quadrilateral considers {{ipc|i}} — {{ipc|æ}} — {{ipc|a̟}} as a straight edge, turning the rectangle into a trapezoid, which looks professional.

A note on sibilant sounds

Sibilant sounds are caused by a grooved tongue shape, which directs the airflow to go through a long narrow channel formed by the tongue, before impacting the roof of the mouth, causing high-pitched noise known as hissing.

Sibilant sounds can be divided into two groups according to their sound quality. First, we have {{ipa|xx|s̪ s ş}}, which sound harsher and brighter, and second, we have {{ipa|xx|ʂ ʆ ʃ ɕ}}, which sound softer and darker. Among them, {{ipa|xx|ş}} and {{ipa|xx|ɕ}} are articulated with the same tongue position, but with a different groove shape, as we will see.

In fact, the difference between the two groups is mainly determined by the position where the airflow, having passed through the groove, impacts the roof of the mouth. For the first group, this position is in front of the alveolar ridge, while for the second group, it is behind the alveolar ridge. This explains the difference between {{ipa|xx|ş}} and {{ipa|xx|ɕ}}: for {{ipa|xx|ş}}, the groove is longer, directing the airflow to go in front of the alveolar ridge, while for {{ipa|xx|ɕ}}, the front part of the tongue is not grooved, and the airflow impacts the back of the alveolar ridge.

Pitch, tones, stress, and length

For pitch accent languages and tonal languages, I only transcribe pitch and tones phonemically. This is because a precise transcription of intonation requires more knowledge than I currently have, and there is no widely recognized notation system for precise transcription of intonation.
